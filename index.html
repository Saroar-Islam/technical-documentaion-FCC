Analysing tools used in stock market
Stock Market

In simple words, stock market is a marketplace where company shares, securities, and other financial instruments are bought, sold, and traded. It is a collection of buyer and seller who trade stocks that represent ownership interests in companies. The stock market operates through a network of exchanges. Companies issue shares of their stock on an exchange, often to raise money to grow their business. Investors invest in those companies through buying share of those companies. Investors then buy and sell these stock among themselves similar to trading in a market. The stock exchange keeps track of supply and demand of every listed stock. Supply and demand aid to determine the price of every share. whenever the demand of a stock goes high the price of that stock tends to rise, and when the demand gets low and there is plenty of supply the price of that stock tends to fall. Trading draws investors with its promise of money and freedom, challenge and excitement. But only way for an investor or a trader to make profit is to predict the market, when prices will go up and when prices will fall so he or she can buy or sell this profitable stocks. But how can an investor predicts a market that is constantly shifting position ? Through analyzing the market, and the factors that might affect the market. If an investor go in blindly, without knowing which stock might be in demand or in supply, and he might lose all his capital.

Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis is a process of predicting intrinsic value of an stock by examining related economic and financial factors that could influence its value in the future. Fundamental analysts study anything that can affect a stock's value, from macroeconomic factors such as the state of the economy and industry conditions to microeconomics factors like the effectiveness of the company's management. A fundamental investor believes, the market price of a stock tends to revert towards its intrinsic value. If intrinsic value of a stock is over the current market price, the investor would purchase the stock because he/she believes that the stock price would rise and move towards its intrinsic value. If intrinsic value of a stock is lower than the market price, the investor would sell the stock since he/she believes that the stock price will fall and come closer to its intrinsic value. To determine the intrinsic value of a company, the fundamental analysis takes a top-down approach. It involves analyzing shares of companies by first analyzing the economy, then the industry, and then the company itself.

Economic Analysis

In the first step of fundamental analyzing involves the study of the overall economy. Here, main aim in to determining if the economic climate is favorable and capable of encouraging the growth of the business sector, especially the capital market. If a company is operating in a market condition that support its operation economically and also will be favorable in the future then the company's value will also be growing. Therefore, to predict share value, an investor has to spend time understanding the forces operating in the overall economy. Economic activities put an effect on corporate profit, not directly through tax policies but also through foreign policies and administrative procedures, so it is essential to predict the direction of the national economy. Zero growth rate of an economy can cause to lower business profits, which will result in a lower share price and investors outlook of the company. Here are some important factors that affect the overall economy:

Economic Growth

Economic growth is calculated by a country's Gross Domestic Product (GDP) or Gross National Product (GNP). The total value of final goods and services produces within a year in known as GDP. Economic growth increases production of goods and services in a country, which increase the consumers spending. The increase in sale results in firm increase their investment spending and fund in hiring, which cause more income in the of consumers, thus resulting more spending.

Interest rate and Inflation

Interest rate is the cost at which consumer and firm borrow money. When interest rate is low, people can borrow more and can spend more, conversely when borrowing rate is high people spend less money. Inflation is the increase in price of overall goods in an economy. When prices are too high, consumers tend to lower their spending which results in firms cutting back on investment spending. For this reason interest rate is used to control inflation, if the interest rate is high, people will be able to borrow less money, spending will decrease as a result economy will slow down and Inflation will decrease. Conversely, if interest rate is low, people will have more money to spend, which will result in a growing economy and Inflation will increase.

Unemployment

Unemployment rates reflects the number of unemployed person in the labor market. When the rate unemployment rate is low more money will go to the labor force, which will end up in consumers hand. And more people will be unemployed less money will to go the consumers, which will result in less spending. And when recession which cause more unemployment companies loses profit, consumers tend to save than spending money because of constant fear of losing jobs.

Consumer Spending and Saving

Employment and interest put an effect on consumer confidence. If the future economic expectation is high, consumer tend to spend more and save less. Conversely when if future economic expectation is low, consumers saves more and spend as little as possible, which put as effect is companies sale.

Capital Goods Market

Capital goods are necessary to produce more consumer goods. But investment in capital goods lower the Standard of living in short run, cause resources are use for capital goods instead of consumers goods. This sacrifice can bring positive result in long run when more consumer goods can be produces and standard of living will improve as a result.

Foreign Direct investment

Foreign Direct investments have different effect on different sectors of economy. If FDI flow in the primary sectors is often puts a negative effect in economic growth, but when FDI flow in the manufacturing sector in put s a positive effect. (Alfaro, 2003)

Monetary Policy

Monetary policy is mainly use for money supply. Factor that monetary policy contribute to the economic growth is price stability through with different tools and money supply.

Tax structure

There is an argument both for and against higher tax result in improve economic growth. Higher tax lead to higher government investment which lead to better infrastructure, improve R&D, and education, which causes economy's production capacity. On the contrary, higher tax prevent the incentive for company to produce more or the individuals spend more or supply more labor.

Industry Analysis

In a certain economic condition not all industry will be in favorable condition. Some will have the advantage over other and some will be undervalued. Industry analysis is the tool that gives analysts necessary information to assess which would be promising and valued company in an industry to invest.

Cyclical Industries

Some industry are linked with the economic growth, and move along with the economy, such as construction, manufacturing and banking industries. These industries depend on the consumer spending. When consumer spend more money they make more sale and if consumers save more they tend to lose business.

Defensive Industries

Contrary to the cyclical industries some industry considered stable in their earnings, poor economic condition doesn't affect them greatly because demand of their goods or services are fairly stable. Utilities, pharmaceuticals and fast moving consumer goods are considered defensive.

Customers

Number of customers of an company vary from industry to industry. Some Sector only supply its product to a hand full of customers, while some has a millions of customers. In term of losing customers those who serve a few will be always in greater risk. A change in economy or a change is tax will wipe out a large amount of sale.

Market Share

Market share indicates company's position in the industry. A high volumes of share means the company is one of the largest player in the market, which will help it to dictate price of its own goods and services as well as negotiate a good price with its suppliers. Also company will be able to collect a sizeable revenue in the industry when the industry boom occurs.

Industry Growth

Company Growth potential can be determine by overall market growth. If there is no new customers, company has to steal customers from its competitors to grow its business. So analyst has to examine the industry to determine its growth and also company's ability to divert customer from its competitors.

Regulation

Some industry are highly regulated by government. Industries like pharmaceutical where company has to go through long testing process before bring a new product in the market. Which cost money and time that add in the company's cost. In few cases profit margin in directly fix by the government. Utilities industries, where one or two companies make up the whole industry. While there is promise of huge profit but it limited by the regulation.

Company Analysis

Company analysis is analysis of the company's fundamentals. Here, analyst or investor evaluate company's profitability, profile and goods or services through analyzing the fundamental factors of a company. Despite a company being in a growing and promising industry it can fail to generate sales or profit. Fundamental factors have a more active role in determining company that has best stock value. Fundamental factors can be grouped into two types, quantitative and qualitative.

Quantitative Factors

Factors that can be measured in numerical term usually we call those quantitative. These are the fundamentals measured in number to evaluate a company asset, liability, revenue, cost, sales, profit, share holders equity etc.

Analyzing Income Statement and Balance Sheet

When investors try to get a knowledge about company's costs, sales and profit margin they look at the income statement of a company. It gives them the idea of company past performance also the growth compare to the overall industry. Revenue growth and earnings growth rate gives the investor the idea about company ability to generate future earnings. Balance sheet keeps track of all the asset and liability of the company. From asset investor can know how much cash flow a company generate within a year. Investor feels confidence when a business holds more cash on hand, as it protect the company in case of poor market condition. To many inventories gives the investor the idea that company cannot generate enough sales, so inventories are piling up. When a company's receivable is increasing, it indicates company is failing to collect payments from customers. And in case of bad debt expense company will lose money. Here are some ratios that investor calculate to get a better picture of a company's fundamentals:

Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios can tell an investor if the company is able to pay of its short-term debt when they arise and in a timely manner. Also it also give the investor the idea about how efficiently a company can convert its current assets into cash so that it doesn't have to look for external capital. Basic liquidity ratios are:

  • Current ratio

    This ratio measure if a company can pay its current liability with its current assets. Current assets are Cash and bank, Inventories, Debtor, receivables, loan and advances, and others. Current Ratio=(Current Assets)/(Current LIabilities) Current liabilities are Creditor, Short-term loan, bank overdraft, outstanding expenses etc. Current ratio include inventories as a current assets. Inventories are not always easy to convert into cash which becomes a problem for company, for this reason company use the Quick Ratio. Usually 2:1 is the ideal ratio for the current ration, but it always vary from industry to industry.

  • Quick Ratio

    Quick ratio is same as the current ratio expect it exclude inventories from current assets. Quick ratios mainly current assets such as cash or assets that can be convert into cash very easily divided by current liabilities.

  • Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)

    DSO refers to the how long is take a company to collect payment from its customers after a sale. The longer it takes a company to collect payment the longer it has to run its operative activities with other sources of capitals. DOS=(Average Accounts recivable )/(Revenue Per Day)

Solvency Ratio

From solvency ratios investors or analyst determine a company's ability to pay of its long term obligations and debts. A solvent company will be able to generate sufficient cash to meet its long- term liabilities. The probability of a company's defaulting on its debt depend on its solvency ratio.

  • Debt to Equity Ratio

    Debt to Equity indicate how much debt company using compare to its shareholder's equity to finance its operations. When a D/E is high is means company is using more creditors money than the shareholders. Which puts company in more debt, and is risky for the shareholders. D/E=(Total Liability )/(Shareholder's Equity)

  • Equity Ratio

    This Ratio indicates how much of a company's assets are being finance with investors money i.e. shareholder's equity. It show what percentage of company's assets are owned by investor and also how leveraged the company is with debt. A high equity ratio is Equity Ratio=(Total Equity)/(Total Assets) always good which show company is in less debt and which will attract more investor for the company. Also new creditors usually doesn't want to give loan to a company with a low equity ratio, for the fear of default.

  • Debt Ratio

    Debt Ratio indicates the financial leverage of a company. It measure the company's ability to pay off all liability by selling all assets. If the liability is higher than assets then the company is highly leveraged. Investor and Creditors use this ratio to measure company's current ability to pay debt also prepare for future in case of economic instability. Debt Ratio=(Total Liabilities)/(Total Assets)

Technical Analysis

Technical analysis is a methodology of predicting future price of stock based on current market action. It collect data from trading activities like price movement and volume to evaluate and determine in which direction the price of that stock will go next. Any security with historical data can be measured by using technical analysis to evaluate its strength and weakness. Stocks, commodities, fixed-income, currencies, and other securities are evaluated with technical analysis. To use technical Analysis investors look into the charts that gives them the price movement and trading volume history of a certain stock, from these charts investor identify trends and changes in those trends. Technical Analysis was first introduced by Charles Dow, who also formulated the Dow theory. After Charles Dow William P. Hamilton, Robert Rhea, Edson Gould and John Magee further developed Dow Theory concepts helping to form its basis. The concept of Dow Theory is the foundation of modern day technical analysis. The concept of Dow theory is that if one of its average climbs to an intermediate high and another average also follow it within reasonable time than the market is in an upward trend.

Charts

In Technical analysis charts represent price information of a stock in graphical from. A combination of price, volume and time intervals based on past data are shown in the charts. Generally charts where created by hand, but nowadays with help of technology they are drawn in computer. There are different type of charts such as line chart, candlestick chart, bar chart, Renko chart, point and figure chart. The of chart a investor or analyst use is depend on the information they want to use as part of their analysis.

  • Line Charts

    These charts are consist a single line connecting only the closing prices of each time period going left to right. Line charts are the basis and most popular form of charts that are used for presenting a general view of past and current direction. While line chart is simple and provide a general view of price movement consisting closing point is each period, many investor want more information from a chart which can provide them with intraday movement of price.

  • Bar charts

    Bar charts contain a series of vertical lines that indicate the price ranges i.e. openings, highs, lows and closings during certain time period. As Bar chart considers opening highs, lows and closing it also called as OHLC chart. Investor can discover the price patterns more easily as bar chart provide them with a intraday price range.

  • Candlestick Chart

    This chart uses a candle like bar to represent price movement. There is two part of a candlestick, main body and shadow. The main body or thick part represent the difference between opening and closing prices where black or red meaning the stock closes on a lower price and if white or green stock closes in higher prices. The top shadow or the thin part of the candlestick indicate high price while bottom shadow indicates lowest price for that day.

  • Renko Chart

    Unlike most other charts Renko Chart contain only price movement. It has no indication of standardized time intervals or volumes. Chart is created by green or white brick when the price move up, and black or red brick for down price. Renko chart is a noiseless charting technique for traders who prefer simple way to identify support and resistances

Basic Principles of Technical Analysis

Technical analysis is use to determine price action for a stock in order to make an investment decision. Before predicting the price movement technical analysis need to evaluate the market actions, for that some basic principles need to be followed.

Bullish

A bullish market or investor's bullish mindset terms are used when stock price most likely to rise. If market moves 10% upward, then the term "Bullish" is use. The term as name suggest came from the bull, who hits upward with its horns.

Bearish

The term "Bearish" represent market price will go down or its already going down. As the name suggest it came from bear, who hits downward with its pawns. Usually bearish represent the sellers in the market, who believes the prices will fall and they start selling.

Support Levels

Support level is specific level that is located below the actual price level, if the price falls and meet the support level, chances are high that it will take a bullish direction and go up. But if the price fall below the support level it will continue to fall to the next lower support level.

Resistance Levels

Resistance level located in the opposite direction of support level. When the price goes up and meet resistance levels, chances are high that it will take a bearish turn. But same as support level is the price break the resistance level it rise till it meet a higher resistance level.

Indicators of Technical Analysis

Simple Moving Average

In technical analysis simple moving average (SMA) is the most popular moving average that is used by investors and analyst. Because of its simplicity and it's very easy to use investor and trader often use SMA over other tool to identify tread direction, it can also be used to generate potential buy or sell signals.
SMA is the average or mean of combination of the closing prices of certain time period then dividing that with that same time period. To understand it in a more simple term let's assume, closing price of a stock for last 5 days was: 70, 71, 65, 68, 70. Now add these prices and divide that with time period, which is 5. (70+71+65+68+70)/5= 68.8. SO 68.8 is the simple moving average for last 5 days.
SMA is commonly used for identify the trend direction. If the SMA line is moving up which indicate there is a upward trend, prices will rise. And if SMA line is moving down, there is downward trend and prices will fall.

Exponential Moving Average

Exponential moving average is similar to simple moving average but it places more weight in the recent data points, it called exponentially weighted moving average. It reacts significantly faster to recent price chances than SMA. Like other moving averages EMA is best used for indentify the direction of trend. So, traders usually use EMA with other indicators to determine the trend and measure the market validity. Day traders or others who use intraday information to analysis the fast moving market often use EMA over other moving average for their buying and selling decisions. When the EMA is going up , traders consider there is a upward trend, and when prices are going below EMA, it considers as a downward trend. Moving averages also helps to indicates support and resistance level. The price action is supported by the rising EMA, while resistance level of the price action can be provided by a falling EMA. This reinforces the strategy of buying when the price is near the rising EMA and selling when the price is near the falling EMA.

Bollinger Bands

Bollinger Bands are volatility indicator that is drawn from one simple moving average and two standard deviation for the purpose of identifying extreme short-term prices chances. The SMA moves along the price and two standard deviation creates two outer bands, standard deviation are used as it is the common measure of volatility. Outer bands are created adding and subtracting one standard deviation from the moving average. Outer bands can also provide support and resistance for a stock. If price is about to hit one outer band usually it bounces back to SMA or even to opposite outer band. When the market is quiet meaning there is low volatility outer bands come closer to the SMA. A narrowing of the bands usually called Bollinger Squeeze, traders use it as entry point before markets gets volatile. When market gets volatile outer bands spread far apart from one another. A low volatility period generally followed by a high volatility period and high volatility followed by low volatility.

Relative Strength Index (RSI)

RSI is oscillator indicator that is developed by J. Welles Wilder. It measure current price strength in relation to previous prices to evaluate if the stock is overbought or oversold. RSI represented as a line that moves between 0 to 100, generally 30 or below is considered as oversold and 70 or above is considered as overbought.
Traders can use RSI as a buy and sell signal, if RSI cross above the oversold line a trader might buy. and if the RSI cross below the overbought line a trader might sell. RSI can also work as divergences. Traders can also use RSI as buy signal when price and RSI both in uptrend and RSI cross below 50 line, conversely it can be use as a sell signals when price and RSI both in downtrend and RSI cross below 50 line

Sentimental Analysis

Besides the Fundamental and Technical analysis there is another fairly new type of analysis that is called Sentimental analysis. It is basically based on traders opinions, market views and instinct. Every trader has their own opinions and ideas about why market is moving in a certain way and how will market react in the future. When all the traders sentiment combine it creates a new sentiment for the market. To understand this market sentiment and trade based on that is essentially sentiment analysis. Let's assume one single trader thinks a certain stock will go bullish, but the whole market is in the bearish mood, despite fundamental and technical what analysis say market will go downward. Same goes for if the market is in the bullish mood, and a single trader thinks prices will fall, others will make profit from the bullish market and that single trader will lose money. So a trader has to combine sentimental analysis with others tools to evaluate the market.
